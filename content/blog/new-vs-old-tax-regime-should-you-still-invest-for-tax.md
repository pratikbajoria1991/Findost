It's ITR season, and this question is everywhere right now: "I'm under the new tax regime — do my ELSS SIPs, PPF, and NPS still save me any tax, or am I wasting money?"

Fair question. Here's the honest, numbers-first answer.

## The new regime is the default now

Since FY 2023-24, the **new tax regime** applies automatically unless you actively opt for the old one. For FY 2025-26 (the year you're filing returns for right now), it's gotten even more generous:

- Basic exemption limit: **₹4 lakh**
- Section 87A rebate: up to **₹60,000**, which means **taxable income up to ₹12 lakh pays zero tax**
- Standard deduction for salaried folks: **₹75,000**, pushing the effective tax-free ceiling to **₹12.75 lakh** of gross salary

But there's a catch: under the new regime, most deductions are gone. No 80C (ELSS, PPF, life insurance), no 80D (health insurance), no HRA, no home loan interest benefit. Section 80CCD(2) — your **employer's** NPS contribution — is one of the few that survives.

## The old regime still rewards disciplined investors

The old regime keeps higher slab rates, but if you actually use the deductions, it can still win:

- **Section 80C**: up to **₹1.5 lakh** — ELSS, PPF, EPF, life insurance premiums, principal on home loan
- **Section 80CCD(1B)**: an extra **₹50,000** for NPS self-contribution, over and above 80C
- **Section 80D**: health insurance premiums, ₹25,000–₹1 lakh depending on age
- **HRA and home loan interest**, if applicable

Rough rule of thumb: if your total claimable deductions cross roughly **₹4–4.5 lakh** a year (80C + NPS + 80D + HRA/home loan combined), the old regime usually comes out ahead. Below that, the new regime's lower slabs typically win. There's no universal answer — you genuinely have to run both numbers for your own salary and deductions before choosing.

## The bigger mistake: stopping ELSS/PPF entirely

Here's what a lot of people get wrong. If you move to the new regime and lose the tax deduction, that doesn't mean the investment itself stopped making sense.

- **ELSS** is still an equity mutual fund with a 3-year lock-in — one of the shortest among tax-saving options, and it participates in equity market growth. Minus the deduction, it's simply... a regular equity fund. If you'd hold equity anyway, ELSS isn't a bad place to keep doing it, especially if you already have running SIPs.
- **PPF** is still a government-backed, tax-free (EEE) instrument for your safe/debt allocation, deduction or not.
- **NPS** still gives you low-cost, long-term retirement exposure, and the employer contribution deduction under 80CCD(2) survives even in the new regime.

The decision to invest and the decision to claim a deduction are two separate questions. Don't let one drive the other.

## A simple way to decide

1. **Calculate your tax under both regimes** using your actual salary, 80C/80D amounts, and HRA — don't guess.
2. **Pick the regime that lowers your total tax outgo** for this year. You can typically switch regimes each year if you're salaried without business income.
3. **Keep investing in ELSS/PPF/NPS based on your goals and asset allocation**, not just the deduction — especially with equity LTCG taxed at **12.5% above ₹1.25 lakh/year** and STCG at **20%**, tax efficiency still matters either way.
