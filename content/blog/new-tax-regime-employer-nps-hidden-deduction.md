If you moved to the new tax regime this year, you probably assumed your tax-saving days were over. No 80C, no HRA exemption, no home loan interest benefit.

But there's one deduction the new regime still allows — and it's sitting quietly in your salary structure, unused, because most people don't know to ask for it.

## The deduction: Section 80CCD(2)

This is different from the NPS deduction you may already know (Section 80CCD(1), where **your own** contribution up to **₹1.5 lakh** gets you a deduction — that one is gone in the new regime).

Section 80CCD(2) is about your **employer's** contribution to your NPS account. And it survives in both tax regimes.

Here's what's changed: earlier, only government employees got the higher limit. Now, if you're on the new regime, **any employee — private or government — can get up to 14% of (Basic + DA) contributed by their employer to NPS, completely tax-free.** Stay on the old regime, and a private-sector employee's limit is capped at 10%.

## What this actually looks like

Say your basic salary is **₹60,000 a month** (₹7.2 lakh a year). At 14%, your employer can route roughly **₹1 lakh a year** into your NPS account as part of your CTC — and none of it is added to your taxable salary.

If you're in the 30% slab, that's about **₹31,200** saved in tax, every year, for money that's still yours and still growing in a retirement account.

## The catch: it's a CTC restructuring, not free money

This isn't extra cash your employer hands you — it comes out of your existing CTC. Typically it replaces a part of your special allowance (which is fully taxable) with an employer NPS contribution (which isn't). Your take-home may look slightly lower on paper, but your tax outgo drops by more than that, so you come out ahead overall.

**Not every company offers this by default.** Many employers only extend it if HR/payroll has set it up as a flexi-benefit option. If yours hasn't, ask — it costs the company nothing extra, since it's a restructuring of what they already pay you.

## Three things to check before opting in

1. **The combined cap.** All employer contributions — NPS, EPF, and superannuation combined — are tax-free only up to **₹7.5 lakh a year** in aggregate. Very high earners with large PF contributions should check they aren't already close to this limit.
2. **It's locked till 60.** NPS is a retirement account. You can withdraw partially only for specific reasons (medical emergencies, home purchase, education), and at exit, a portion must go into an annuity. Don't route money here that you'll need in the next 5-10 years.
3. **NPS is market-linked.** Depending on the fund choice, a meaningful chunk can be in equity. Historically, NPS equity allocations have delivered strong long-term (10+ year) returns, but there's no guaranteed return — treat it like any other market-linked investment, not a fixed deposit.

## Should you opt in?

If you're salaried, in the new regime, and your employer allows it, this is close to a free tax cut — you're just choosing to receive part of your existing salary in a more tax-efficient form. The main trade-off is liquidity: the money is locked away for retirement.
