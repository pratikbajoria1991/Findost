Say you just got a bonus, an FD matured, or you sold a property and have **₹5 lakh** sitting in your bank account. Nifty is still well off its January highs. Do you put it all in today, or wait for things to "settle"?

This is one of the most common questions we're getting right now — and there's no single right answer, but there is a sensible framework.

## Why timing a lump sum feels so hard

With a SIP, the decision is automatic — the same amount goes in every month, rain or shine. A lump sum is different. You're choosing one entry point, and that one decision can weigh on your mind for months.

The honest truth: nobody, including professional fund managers, can reliably call the bottom of a correction. Trying to guess it usually means one of two mistakes — investing right before another dip, or waiting so long that you miss the recovery entirely.

## Three ways to deploy a lump sum

1. **Invest it all today.** If your horizon is long (7+ years) and you can genuinely ignore short-term swings, historical data on Indian equities shows that investing immediately has usually outperformed waiting — because markets spend more years going up than down. This suits investors with strong risk appetite and a long goal.

2. **Use an STP (Systematic Transfer Plan).** Park the full amount in a liquid or ultra-short debt fund, then set up an STP to move a fixed amount into your chosen equity fund every week or month over, say, **6-12 months**. This is the middle path: your money starts earning debt-fund returns immediately instead of sitting idle, while you still get rupee-cost averaging into equity. Many investors use this specifically during corrections like the current one.

3. **Wait and average in manually.** Some prefer to invest in tranches themselves — say, 25% now and the rest over the next few quarters. This works, but be honest with yourself about whether you'll actually follow through, or whether "waiting for a better time" becomes an excuse that drags on for years.

## A simple way to decide

- **Money needed within 3 years?** Don't put it in equity at all — a short-term debt fund or FD is the right home for it, correction or no correction.
- **Money needed in 3-7 years?** Lean towards an STP into a diversified or hybrid fund rather than one shot.
- **Money you won't touch for 7+ years, and you can sleep fine watching it dip further?** Investing it now, or over 2-3 months, both work reasonably well — the difference between the two, historically, has been small.

## The tax angle people forget

However you invest, remember the clock starts fresh for that money:

- For equity mutual funds, gains within **12 months** are **short-term** and taxed at **20%**.
- Hold beyond 12 months, and gains become **long-term**, taxed at **12.5%** — but only on gains above **₹1.25 lakh** in a financial year, which is tax-free.
- If you use an STP, each transfer is treated as a fresh investment for tax purposes, so your units will mature into "long-term" status in batches, not all at once. That's normal and not something to plan around obsessively.

None of this changes what fund or category to pick — a large-cap, flexi-cap, or hybrid fund suited to your goal remains the starting point, not the tax rule.

## The bottom line

A market correction doesn't change whether you should invest — it only affects your entry price, and that matters far less over a 7-10 year horizon than most people assume. What matters more is matching the money to its time horizon and actually starting, instead of waiting for a "perfect" entry point that nobody can identify in advance.
