title: Index funds or active mutual funds — which should you actually choose?
date: 2026-07-19
description: Index funds cost less and mostly win on large-caps. Here's when an active fund still earns its higher fee.
---

If you've spent any time on investing forums this week, you've seen this fight play out again: index funds versus actively managed mutual funds. Everyone has an opinion. Here's what actually matters for your money.

**What's the real difference?**

An index fund (like a Nifty 50 or Nifty 500 fund) simply buys every stock in that index, in the same proportion. No fund manager is picking stocks — it just mirrors the market.

An active fund has a manager who researches and picks stocks, trying to beat that same index. For that effort, you pay more.

**The cost gap is the first thing to notice**

- Direct-plan index funds typically charge an expense ratio of **0.15%–0.20%** a year.
- Direct-plan active equity funds usually charge **1%–2%** a year.

On a **₹10 lakh** portfolio, that's the difference between paying roughly **₹1,500–2,000** a year and paying **₹10,000–20,000** a year — every single year, regardless of how the fund performs. Over 15-20 years of compounding, that fee gap alone can eat into your corpus by several lakh rupees.

**Does the extra fee buy better returns?**

Not reliably, and not everywhere. Two things are both true:

1. In **large-cap** funds, most active managers struggle to beat the Nifty 50 or Nifty 100 consistently over 5+ years, after fees. The large-cap segment is closely tracked by analysts, so there's little informational edge left to exploit.
2. In **mid-cap and small-cap** segments, India's market is less researched and less efficient. A genuinely skilled manager has historically had more room to add value here — though this is never guaranteed, and past outperformance doesn't assure future outperformance.

**Does the choice change your tax bill?**

No — and this trips people up. Whether you hold an index fund or an active equity fund, the taxation is identical:

- **Short-term gains** (held under 1 year): taxed at a flat **20%**.
- **Long-term gains** (held over 1 year): taxed at **12.5%**, but only on gains above **₹1.25 lakh** in a financial year — the first ₹1.25 lakh of long-term gains is tax-free.

So don't pick a fund category hoping for a tax advantage. There isn't one here. The fee and the manager's skill are the only variables that matter.

**A practical way to decide, not a stock tip**

Most first-time investors do well with a simple structure rather than agonising over every choice:

- Use a **low-cost index fund** for your large-cap / core allocation — you're not paying extra for something a manager is unlikely to beat anyway.
- Consider an **active fund** for mid-cap, small-cap, or flexi-cap allocation, if you're comfortable with the added risk and want to give a manager room to add value.
- Whatever mix you pick, don't hold 8-10 overlapping funds "to diversify" — 3-4 funds across categories is usually plenty for most SIP portfolios.

This isn't about finding the one "best" fund — it's about not overpaying for something you can get cheaply, while leaving room for genuine skill where the market rewards it.
