If you have an NPS account, you've probably heard the headline: **"NPS now allows 100% equity!"** And the question that follows is obvious — *"Should I move my whole NPS into it?"*

Here's what the new rule actually says, and why the headline hides an important catch.

## What actually changed

Since **October 2025**, the pension regulator (PFRDA) introduced something called the **Multiple Scheme Framework (MSF)**. Under the old system (Active Choice), your equity exposure in NPS was capped at **75%**, with the rest split across corporate bonds, government securities, and a small alternative-assets sleeve.

Under MSF, pension fund managers can now launch new scheme variants — including a **"high-risk" option that allows up to 100% equity**.

Sounds great for anyone with a long runway to retirement. But here's what most headlines skip.

## The catch nobody mentions

1. **Your existing NPS corpus can't just convert.** You cannot dial up your current account to 100% equity. To access this option, you have to open a **fresh scheme** under MSF — only your *new* contributions going forward flow into it. Money you've already built up stays exactly where it is.
2. **A 15-year minimum vesting period applies** to these new MSF schemes. During this window, you can move out to a standard/default NPS scheme if you change your mind, but you can't freely hop between MSF schemes.
3. **The exit rules don't change.** NPS still requires you to use a chunk of the final corpus to buy an annuity at retirement — the tax-free lump-sum and mandatory annuity structure that applies to your regular NPS account applies here too. A 100% equity sleeve doesn't turn NPS into a fully liquid, exit-anytime investment.

So it's less "make your NPS more aggressive" and more "start a brand-new, higher-risk pension scheme alongside your old one."

## Is it actually worth it?

For a genuinely long horizon — think **20+ years to retirement** — a higher equity allocation has historically compounded faster than a 75%-cap portfolio, simply because equity has more room to work over decades. NPS also comes with a real cost advantage: expense ratios are capped near **0.3%**, well below what most actively managed equity mutual funds charge.

But weigh this against what NPS already asks of you:

- Your money stays **locked in until 60**, with only limited partial withdrawals allowed.
- A part of the corpus must eventually go into an **annuity**, whose payouts are taxed at your slab rate later.
- You're now managing **two schemes** under one umbrella instead of one, which adds a bit of tracking overhead.

If you already have runway of 15-20+ years and are comfortable with the illiquidity, opting fresh contributions into the high-equity MSF scheme can make sense as part of your retirement sleeve. If retirement is closer, or you want a debt/equity mix you can rebalance freely, a mix of index funds and PPF/debt funds outside NPS may suit you better — those don't lock you into an annuity at the end.

## The real decision

Don't ask "should NPS be 100% equity." Ask: **"What role does NPS play in my overall retirement plan, and does adding a high-equity sleeve fit that role?"** Your NPS allocation should be one piece of a broader plan that includes your EPF, mutual funds, and other retirement assets — not evaluated in isolation.
